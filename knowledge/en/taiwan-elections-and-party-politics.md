# Taiwan Elections and Party Politics

> **30-second overview:** On March 23, 1996, 21 million Taiwanese cast ballots in their first direct presidential election, with a 76.04% turnout rate. From the first provincial assembly election in 1950 to party alternation, Taiwan took 50 years to complete its democratic transition. The key wasn't institutional design, but that each generation had people willing to pay the price for democracy.

On the evening of December 10, 1979, riot police faced off against demonstrators on Zhongshan Road in Kaohsiung, tear gas filling the air. This "Kaohsiung Incident" (also known as the Formosa Incident) sent eight opposition leaders including Shih Ming-teh and Lin Yi-hsiung to prison. But unexpectedly, the young lawyers who defended them—Chen Shui-bian, Frank Hsieh, and Su Tseng-chang—all became presidents or premiers 20 years later.

Taiwan's democracy was accumulated vote by vote, generation by generation. Each election witnessed this island's political awakening.

## Democratic Experiments Under Authoritarianism (1950-1979)

### 1950: Provincial Assembly Elections and "Free China"

On June 16, 1950, Taiwan Provincial Assembly held its first elected assembly member election. Thirty electoral districts elected 55 members—this was postwar Taiwan's first democratic election. The turnout reached 78.26%, showing enthusiastic public participation.

But this democracy had many limitations: you could only elect provincial assembly members, not the governor; you could criticize local policies but not question the central government; and you certainly couldn't organize opposition parties. The Kuomintang (KMT) needed to prove to the United States that Taiwan was "Free China," different from the communist regime on the mainland, so they began their "democratic showcase" experiment under the Cold War framework.

The 1954 Taipei mayoral election was a crucial turning point. Wu San-lien defeated KMT candidate Wang Min-ning with 51,896 votes versus 49,417—the first time a non-KMT person won important office since the war. Though Wu San-lien himself had KMT background, this victory proved voters wouldn't follow blindly, opening the era of electoral competition.

### Gradual Expansion of Local Self-Government

Starting in the 1960s, more positions opened to popular election. In 1964, Taipei was restructured as a special municipality with an elected mayor. In 1968, county magistrates and city mayors became popularly elected, requiring local politicians to face voter scrutiny.

This period produced many local political stars. Henry Kao Yu-shu served three consecutive terms as Taipei mayor (1954-1964), earning recognition for governance. Yilan County Magistrate Chen Jin-dong and Pingtung County Magistrate Xu Hong-zhi displayed political styles different from the central government.

More importantly was the rise of "dangwai" (outside the party) figures. After Lei Zhen's failed attempt to organize the China Democratic Party in 1960, opposition figures who couldn't form parties called themselves "dangwai." They participated in elections, proposed different platforms, and challenged KMT one-party dominance.

## The Rise of Dangwai: Challenging Authoritarianism (1975-1986)

### 1975: The Guo Yu-xin Phenomenon

On December 20, 1975, during vote counting for supplementary Legislative Yuan elections, 67-year-old Guo Yu-xin from Yilan won by a landslide. Though specific vote tallies are hard to verify, his victory held landmark significance—he was the first candidate openly criticizing martial law and demanding party legalization to be elected to the Legislative Yuan.

Guo Yu-xin was called the "Grandfather of Dangwai." During his time in the Provincial Assembly, he was known for speaking boldly, often questioning government policies in session. When he ran for legislator in 1975 at nearly 70, he displayed remarkable charisma. His victory showed public dissatisfaction with authoritarian rule, marking the official start of the dangwai movement.

In the 1977 "Five Public Office Elections," dangwai candidates performed even better. They won 4 out of 21 county magistrate and mayoral seats, and 21 out of 77 Taiwan Provincial Assembly seats, garnering about 20% of votes and becoming an undeniable political force.

### 1979: Birth of the Formosa Generation

On August 16, 1979, Formosa Magazine was founded, with Huang Shin-chieh as publisher and Xu Xin-liang as president. This wasn't just a political publication but the organizational center for dangwai figures. They established 10 service centers island-wide, operating virtually as a proto-party.

Formosa boldly challenged political taboos, demanding the lifting of martial law, party legalization, complete parliamentary re-election, and even discussing Taiwan's future. Within three months of founding, circulation exceeded 100,000—an astonishing achievement at the time.

On December 10, International Human Rights Day, Formosa Magazine held a human rights commemoration in Kaohsiung that escalated into bloody conflict. Military police cracked down heavily, arresting eight dangwai leaders: Huang Shin-chieh, Shih Ming-teh, Yao Chia-wen, Lin Yi-hsiung, Annette Lu, Chen Chu, Zhang Jun-hong, and Xu Xin-liang.

But the Formosa Incident unexpectedly cultivated a group of political talent. The defense attorneys Chen Shui-bian, Frank Hsieh, Su Tseng-chang, Zhang Jun-xiong, and You Qing, plus family members of the arrested like Xu Rong-shu and Zhou Qing-yu, all later became pillars of the democratic movement. This "Formosa Generation" successively entered elections in the 1980s, injecting new blood into Taiwan's democracy.

## Lifting Martial Law and Party Formation (1986-1996)

### September 28, 1986: Birth of the DPP

On the afternoon of September 28, 1986, over 130 dangwai figures gathered in the Dunmu Hall on the 10th floor of Taipei's Grand Hotel. At the "1986 Dangwai Election National Support Association" meeting, You Qing and Frank Hsieh proposed forming a party, gaining signed support from attendees and officially announcing the establishment of the "Democratic Progressive Party" (DPP).

Taiwan was still under martial law then, making party formation illegal. But the Chiang Ching-kuo government chose tolerance—a decision of epochal significance. In the Legislative Yuan election two months later, the DPP got 28.9% of votes and won 12 seats. This exceeded expectations, proving Taiwan society's desire for two-party politics.

### July 15, 1987: Lifting Martial Law

At midnight on July 15, 1987, martial law—in effect for 38 years and 56 days—was officially lifted. Freedom of speech, assembly, association, and party organization were now protected, and Taiwan entered the era of true democratic competition.

The first county magistrate and mayoral elections after martial law were held on December 2, 1989, with unprecedented fierce competition. The DPP won 6 out of 21 county and city executive seats with 37.8% of votes. More importantly, this election featured the first televised debates, allowing candidates to face off directly. TV debates became standard equipment for Taiwan elections thereafter.

### 1996: The Year of Direct Presidential Elections

On March 23, 1996, Taiwan held its first direct presidential election in history—a watershed in the Republic of China's constitutional history and the first direct presidential election in any Chinese society.

Four tickets ran: Lee Teng-hui/Lien Chan (KMT), Peng Ming-min/Frank Hsieh (DPP), Lin Yang-kang/Hau Pei-tsun, and Chen Lu-an/Wang Qing-feng. Lee Teng-hui ultimately won with 5.81 million votes and 54.00%, with 76.04% turnout.

Before the election, the Chinese Communist Party launched missiles threatening Taiwan, unexpectedly uniting all sectors of society. Though parties had different platforms, all supported democratic elections. This "baptism of democracy amid missile crisis" made Taiwan's democratic system more solid.

## Party Alternation Becomes the Norm (2000-2024)

### 2000: The First Party Alternation

On March 18, 2000, Chen Shui-bian was elected president with 39.30% of votes, bringing the DPP to power for the first time and ending 55 years of KMT rule. More importantly, the power transfer was completely peaceful. On election night, defeated candidate Lien Chan accepted the results and wished the new government well, displaying democratic grace.

At inauguration, Chen Shui-bian proposed the "Four Noes and One Without": no independence declaration, no change of national title, no insertion of the state-to-state theory into the constitution, no referendum on unification or independence, and no abolition of the National Unification Guidelines. This pragmatic attitude stabilized the political situation.

### Normalization of Alternation

In 2008, Ma Ying-jeou was elected with 58.45%, returning the KMT to power and completing the second party alternation. In 2016, Tsai Ing-wen was elected with 56.12%, bringing the DPP back to power for the third alternation. In 2024, Lai Ching-te was elected, giving the DPP a third consecutive term.

All these alternations followed the same pattern: fair electoral competition, accepted results, peaceful power transfer. Party alternation evolved from "historic events" to "normal phenomena"—precisely the mark of mature democracy.

## Taiwan's Electoral Culture

### Campaign Rallies and Political Carnivals

Taiwan elections' biggest feature is boisterous campaign rallies. In the 1994 Taipei mayoral election, Chen Shui-bian held a 10,000-person rally at Chiang Kai-shek Memorial Hall, pioneering large-scale rallies. Since then, every major election sees candidates competing on rally scale.

Rallies aren't just political activities but community carnivals. Candidates invite entertainers to perform and distribute boxed meals, towels, and other gifts. Terms like "dongzuan" (Taiwanese pronunciation of "elected"), "Han Wave," and "Ko fans" became political symbols, showcasing Taiwanese linguistic creativity.

### Television Debates and Rational Elections

The first TV debate in the 1994 Taipei mayoral election became standard equipment for major elections thereafter. Good debate performance could reverse fortunes, like Ko Wen-je in the 2014 Taipei mayoral debate; poor performance could devastate campaigns.

The internet era further changed electoral ecology. Candidates communicate directly with voters through social media, with young voters becoming crucial. The 2014 Sunflower Student Movement and 2018 "Han Wave" phenomenon showed the internet's massive electoral influence.

## Challenges to Democracy

Taiwan's democracy faces new challenges. Disinformation problems are increasingly serious, especially information operations from overseas. Money politics remains a concern—though laws provide regulation, loopholes persist.

Generational differences bring new issues. Young voters care more about issues and don't blindly follow parties. This helps rationalize politics but requires parties to keep pace with the times.

Looking back at Taiwan's 70+ year electoral history, from the 1950 Provincial Assembly to the 2024 presidential election, we see how a society moved from authoritarianism to democracy through ballots. Each vote was an accumulation of democratic consciousness; each election was institutional improvement.

Taiwan's democratic story continues. Facing new challenges requires continuously perfecting institutions and improving democratic quality. But one thing is certain: democracy has taken deep root in this land, becoming the most precious common asset of the Taiwanese people.
