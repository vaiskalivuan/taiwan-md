# Stanley Yen: From the Mailroom to Taiwan's Godfather of Tourism — Then He Gave It All Up

> **30-second overview:** Stanley Yen (嚴長壽) has only a high school diploma. At 23, he joined American Express Taiwan as a mailroom clerk. At 28, he became its General Manager — the first Taiwanese person to hold that position. At 32, he was named president of The Landis Taipei, which he built into one of Asia's premier boutique hotels. His debut book sold 600,000 copies. Then, at 62, he resigned from everything to work on education in Taiwan's remote east coast. The following year, he was diagnosed with kidney cancer and lost a kidney. In March 2025, at 78, he opened an art center on the Taitung coast that took 12 years to build. His life arc is a compressed history of Taiwan's own evolution — from proving itself to the world, to asking what comes after success.

In 1970, a 23-year-old graduate of Keelung High School walked into the offices of American Express Taiwan. The position he applied for was _chuanda xiaodi_ (傳達小弟) — literally "message boy." Collecting mail, delivering documents, running errands[^1].

In that era, foreign companies in Taiwan reserved management for expatriates. Local staff handled administration. Nobody expected a young Taiwanese man without a university degree to rise to anything significant.

Five years later, this mailroom clerk became General Manager of American Express Taiwan — the first Taiwanese person to hold that title[^2]. What did he do? He wrote a marketing plan that turned a money-losing branch into a profitable one. No MBA, no overseas credentials, no family connections. What he had was a singular ability: seeing needs that others hadn't noticed yet, and meeting them.

His name is Stanley Yen. People would later call him Taiwan's "Godfather of Tourism" (觀光教父).

## The Landis Taipei: A Taiwanese Definition of "World-Class"

In 1979, hotel developer Chou Chih-jung invited Yen to serve as president of the newly opened Landis Taipei (亞都麗緻大飯店)[^3]. He was 32.

At the time, Taiwan's luxury hotels uniformly imitated the West — European lobbies, Western restaurants, English-language service. Yen did something counterintuitive: he put Taiwanese culture inside a luxury hotel. Local artists' work hung in the lobby. The restaurant served refined Taiwanese cuisine. Room design incorporated Chinese cultural elements[^4].

This was a risk in 1980s Taiwan. Guests at luxury hotels expected Paris and New York, not Taipei. But Yen's logic was clear: if international guests wanted the Paris experience, they'd go to Paris. They came to Taiwan to experience Taiwan.

The Landis Taipei was eventually rated among Asia's finest boutique hotels by the international hospitality press. Yen had proved something: "world-class" didn't require imitation. It could be self-defined.

## 600,000 Copies: How a Hotel President Became a National Mentor

In 1997, Yen published _The Lion Heart of a CEO_ (《總裁獅子心》)[^5]. A management book by a hotel executive — not an obvious bestseller.

It sold over 600,000 copies, making it one of the best-selling business books in Taiwan's publishing history[^6].

Why? Because Yen wasn't really writing about management. He was writing about attitude. "Service is not a humble job — it is a noble calling." In 1990s Taiwan — a society that still viewed service work as "serving others" in the demeaning sense — this message carried enormous impact. His own story, from mailroom to boardroom, proved that no degree or background was required, only the commitment to do every small thing with excellence.

In 2008, _The Future I See_ (《我所看見的未來》) analyzed Taiwan's crises and opportunities with rare foresight. It was selected as required reading for civil servants[^7]. In 2011, _Education Should Be Different_ (《教育應該不一樣》) challenged the structural problems of Taiwan's exam-driven education system[^8].

From hotel service to national futures — his books moved steadily further from the hospitality industry. In retrospect, they were previews of his life's second act.

## The Resignation: After Typhoon Morakot

In August 2009, Typhoon Morakot devastated southern and eastern Taiwan. Yen traveled to Taitung to see the damage[^9].

What he saw wasn't just a typhoon's aftermath. He saw the condition of rural children — under-resourced schools, absent teachers, outdated textbooks. While urban kids attended cram schools and learned English, rural kids sat in crumbling classrooms facing a world that wouldn't wait for them.

On December 28, 2009, Yen founded The Alliance Cultural Foundation (公益平台文化基金會) and committed himself fully to education in Hualien and Taitung[^10]. He stepped away from the daily operations of The Landis Taipei — a man at the peak of a thirty-year hospitality career, walking away from every title and comfort he had earned.

> **📝 Curator's Note**
> Yen didn't completely sever ties with The Landis Taipei — he retained the title of board chairman until formally stepping down in 2016[^11]. But from 2009 onward, virtually all his time and energy went into Taiwan's east coast.

## Junyi School: Master Hsing Yun's Seed, Yen's Tree

In 2008, Fo Guang Shan founder Master Hsing Yun (星雲大師) established Junyi Elementary and Middle School in Taitung[^12]. In 2011, Master Hsing Yun invited Yen to take over as board chairman with full authority to restructure the school's direction.

Yen transformed Junyi from an ordinary rural school into an educational laboratory. Indigenous cultural preservation, place-based ecological learning, and visiting artists and musicians from Taiwan and abroad became central pillars. In 2019, the Ministry of Education officially recognized Junyi as an "International Experimental Education School"[^13].

Most Junyi students come from local Taitung families, including many Indigenous children. Yen's educational philosophy: don't measure Taitung's children by Taipei's standards. Rural communities aren't "underdeveloped cities" — they're places with their own land-based wisdom and cultural depth. The purpose of education isn't to turn rural children into urbanites; it's to help them find strength in their own soil.

## 2010: Cancer

In the afterword of his 2011 book, Yen revealed that in 2010 he had been diagnosed with kidney cancer. The tumor was located centrally, making partial removal impossible. An entire kidney was removed[^14].

He didn't stop. After the surgery, he adjusted his diet — soy milk, eggs, and papaya in the morning; vegetables at lunch; fish in the evening — and continued traveling across Hualien and Taitung for the schools, the foundation, and everything he considered more important than his own health[^15].

On October 25, 2011, he received the Third-Class Brilliant Star Medal (三等景星勳章) at the Presidential Office[^16].

A man with only a high school diploma, from the mailroom to the Presidential Office. Between those two points: American Express, The Landis Taipei, 600,000 books sold, a typhoon, a school, and one missing kidney.

## The Art Center at Jinzun: His Latest Work at 78

On March 15, 2025, the Paul Chiang Art Center (江賢二藝術園區) opened in Jinzun, Taitung[^17].

This was a project Yen had championed for 12 years. Paul Chiang (江賢二) is one of Taiwan's most important abstract painters, who settled in Taitung in his later years. Yen convinced him to build an art center on the east coast shoreline — not in Taipei, not in any commercial district, but in one of the most remote stretches of Taiwan's Pacific coast.

Yen serves as the center's director. At 78, minus one kidney, he's still building things for Taitung.

## Three Lives in One

Yen's life divides into three acts:

**Act One (age 23-62): Proving himself.** From the mailroom to the presidency, from no degree to 600,000 copies sold. The driving question: "How far can someone go who wasn't supposed to go anywhere?"

**Act Two (age 62-78): Giving back to the land.** Resigning from every title, going to Taitung to build schools, promote art, connect local culture. The driving question: "What can I still do for this land?"

**The thread connecting both:** What he did at The Landis (defining "world-class" through Taiwanese culture) and what he does at Junyi (defining "good education" through local wisdom) share the same logic — **don't measure yourself by someone else's standards. You can define what "good" means.**

Perhaps that's why a man with only a high school diploma could get this far. He was never on anyone else's coordinate system.
